§ 05 / Generational Read

Who this teaching reaches first.

Based on the topic mix, scripture footprint, and message style, here's a likely generational read of who Central's teaching slate is most naturally pulling toward, and who needs more intentionality.

Strongest Pull

The current slate skews toward Millennial parents and Gen X. Heavy use of relationships, identity, emotional health, generosity, and discipleship maps directly to the spiritual questions a 30-55-year-old is asking, particularly someone in a family system, navigating career, and considering "is the faith I inherited still mine?"

Resonant Strengths

  • ▸ Mental health series hits a Gen Z trigger (42% cite mental health as a revival driver)
  • ▸ Mission & justice emphasis (Love Beyond, MOHI) aligns with Gen Z's stated hunger for tangible impact
  • ▸ "What's the Deal With?" apologetics arc is exactly the move Gen Z is asking for, evidence and intellectual honesty
  • ▸ Rest & rhythm content speaks directly to burnout, a top-tier issue in 2025-26 Barna data

Likely Gaps

Several themes that current research surfaces as load-bearing for Gen Z and emerging adults are under-represented in the slate. None of these are absent, but the cumulative weight is light.

  • ▸ Holy Spirit / experiential faith at 3.8%, Gen Z's gravitation toward embodied, ancient practice suggests room here
  • ▸ Prayer as practice at 3.8%, only one explicit prayer series across five years
  • ▸ Suffering & lament at 4.2%, the spiritual openness Barna is tracking emerges from dislocation, not optimism
  • ▸ Old Testament narrative at 13% of book references, Gen Z is biblically under-formed and curious
  • ▸ AI & technology discipleship, not yet covered; Barna 2026 flags 1 in 3 adults treating AI as pastoral advisor
§ 06 / Recommendations

Five moves for the next ministry year.

If the teaching calendar were a portfolio, these are the rebalancing trades the data suggests. Each is shaped by the gap between our slate and what current research says the people in front of us are asking.

01
Add an Old Testament narrative series each year.
87% of our scripture references are New Testament. The OT books we touched (Jonah, Malachi, Psalms, Nehemiah, Proverbs) all landed well, suggesting the appetite is there. A six-week walk through Genesis 1-11, Exodus, or the life of David would close the gap and serve biblically under-formed younger adults.
02
Build a standing prayer rhythm into the calendar.
Prayer appears in only 3.8% of sermons, mostly as a single message inside a broader series. Gen Z research consistently surfaces a hunger for ancient, embodied practice. A dedicated four-week prayer series, Lord's Prayer, lament, intercession, listening prayer, would meet a stated cultural need and shore up an obvious gap.
03
Teach suffering and lament before crisis forces it.
Suffering, grief, and lament show up in only 4.2% of sermons, mostly within mental-health series. Barna's data on rising spiritual openness ties directly to dislocation and loss. A standalone series on biblical lament (Psalms, Job, Lamentations) would give people categories before they need them and validate what they're already feeling.
04
Plant a "Faith & Tech" thread.
1 in 3 US adults, 2 in 5 Gen Z and Millennials, say AI spiritual advice is as trustworthy as a pastor's. This is a discipleship issue, not just a culture-war issue. Two to four messages on digital formation, attention, AI as a spiritual companion, and what it means to be embodied in a disembodying age would put us ahead of where most non-denominational churches will land in 2027.
05
Lean further into apologetics, make it permanent.
"What's the Deal With?" was a strong move. The data says Gen Z is open to Jesus but cautious about organized religion and wants evidence, honesty, and room to wrestle. Make a question-driven apologetics block a fixed feature each year, perhaps spring, between Easter and summer, rather than a one-off experiment.
06
Track topical balance forward.
This atlas is retrospective. The same taxonomy could be applied to next year's calendar before series are locked, giving the teaching team a live dashboard of what's getting weight and what's getting starved. Pair it with attendance and decision data when you have it, and it becomes a stewardship tool, not just an audit.
