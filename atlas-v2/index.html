Executive Summary

The data, the peers, and the five trades worth making.

Mesa's teaching slate over the past five years is consistent with how peer non-denominational churches (North Point, Life.Church, Saddleback, NCC, Highlands) structure their preaching calendar. The pattern is solid. The gaps are specific and addressable.

Three findings from the retrospective: The slate is faith-centered and discipleship-driven. Mental health and emotional wellness is a Mesa strength versus peers. The Old Testament is heavily under-weighted (87/13 NT/OT), and four specific peer-norm categories are light: prayer as practice, suffering and lament, dedicated marriage and family teaching, and the (emerging but not yet permanent) apologetics arc.

One finding from the peer landscape: Nearly every comparable church runs a hybrid 8-12 series annual model anchored to five fixed calendar moments: January Vision & Fresh-Start, Lent/Easter, Summer Bible Book, Fall Launch, and Advent. Mesa already runs four of these well. The most common gap among Mesa-like churches that move from good to great is locking in the fifth, a January Pray First / 21 Days of Prayer pattern that Highlands, Watermark, and Transformation Church all use.

The proposed 2026-27 calendar (Section 06) plugs four specific moves into Mesa's existing rhythm to close those gaps without disrupting what already works: a Genesis 1-11 fall narrative series, a January Pray First initiative, a "Faith & Tech" three-week arc, and a dedicated lament series post-Easter.

§ 02b / The Read

Strengths to protect, gaps to close.

The matrix simplified. Two columns: what Mesa is doing better than the peer average, and where the next planning cycle has the most room to move.

Strengths to protect

  • Mental Health & Emotional Wellness Through the Valley (2021) and The Battle Within (2024-25). Barna 2026 says 42% of Gen Z cite mental health as the #1 revival catalyst. Few peers have Mesa's sustained track record. 21 sermons · 8.0%
  • Mission, Evangelism & Justice Love Beyond / MOHI / Matthew 25 work. Maps directly to Gen Z's hunger for tangible impact. 54 sermons · 20.6%
  • Hope, Fresh Start & Endurance Through the Valley, GRIT, post-Easter "Why Jesus" content. 50 sermons · 19.1%
  • Discipleship & Spiritual Growth Anchored by Jesus First / Jesus Manifesto / Crossroads. 35 sermons · 13.4%
  • Rest & Sabbath Healthy Hustle hit a top-tier Barna 2025 theme (burnout). 13 sermons · 5.0%

Gaps to close

  • OT Narrative & Bible Book Walks 87/13 NT/OT split. Only six OT books touched in five years. Largest gap. 0 dedicated OT narrative series
  • Prayer as Practice Only one explicit prayer-themed message across the period. Peers run an annual January 21 Days pattern. 10 sermons · 3.8%
  • Suffering & Lament Mostly inside mental health series. Lament as a biblical category (Psalms, Job, Lamentations) has barely surfaced. 11 sermons · 4.2%
  • Doubt / Apologetics Emerging via "What's the Deal With?" Make it annual and permanent. 10 sermons · 3.8%
  • Marriage & Family (dedicated) Carried mostly by Mother's/Father's Day stand-alones. No dedicated series in the 5-year window. 15 sermons · 5.7% (mostly incidental)
  • AI & Tech Discipleship Not yet covered. Barna 2026: 1 in 3 adults treat AI spiritual advice as comparable to a pastor's. 0 sermons

§ 06 / Unified Recommendations

Eight moves, in priority order.

Combining the audit-side recommendations from V1 with the planning-side recommendations from the peer-landscape research. Ordered by impact-to-effort ratio.

01
Add an annual Old Testament narrative series.
The largest single gap. Recommended for fall 2026: Genesis 1-11 (6 weeks). Origin stories draw fall attenders and serve biblically under-formed younger adults. Model: Highlands "In the Beginning God."
02
Launch a January Pray First / 21 Days of Prayer pattern.
Closes the prayer gap (3.8%) and gives Mesa the missing fifth anchor. Three-week series paired with a 21-day fast. Highlands, Watermark, and Transformation Church all use this exact pattern; it has become the de facto January convention.
03
Lock the calendar grid before brainstorming topics.
Decide the number of series (9-12), week counts (4-6 each), and breather weeks (1-3) first. Then fill in topics. This prevents "we love this idea, let's stretch it to 9 weeks" topic creep.
04
Make apologetics permanent, not experimental.
"What's the Deal With?" was a strong move in 2025-26. Lock a question-driven apologetics block into the spring calendar each year. Gen Z is open to Jesus but cautious about organized religion, evidence and intellectual honesty are exactly what they ask for.
05
Plant a "Faith & Tech" thread.
Barna 2026: 1 in 3 adults treat AI as pastoral advisor (2 in 5 for Gen Z/Millennial). Three messages on digital formation, AI as spiritual companion, and embodiment puts Mesa ahead of where most peers will land in 2027.
06
Teach lament before crisis forces it.
Standalone four-week series on biblical lament (Psalms of lament, Job, Lamentations). Gives people categories before they need them. Barna frames revival as "emerging from dislocation." Pair with the rising mental health work.
07
Adopt one tension-question subtitle per topical series.
North Point is the gold standard. Every series carries a felt-need tension: "Future Family, what does your preferred future look like?" Drives promotion, sticks in people's heads, and forces sermon clarity. Adopt as a teaching team convention.
08
Audit topical rotation annually using this matrix.
NCC categorizes every series under category buckets and checks at year-end whether anything was skipped. The rotation matrix in Section 02 is that tool for Mesa. Re-run it each May before the next year locks.
